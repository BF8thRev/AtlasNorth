# Newton Copywriting Style Guide

**Source:** Copy of Copywriting Style Guide | Newton (G-Drive: Newton Background Folder)
**Updated:** 2026-03-06
**Category:** [Newton] — Communication Standards

---

## Tone (Never Deviate)

- Calm
- Direct
- Observational
- Unimpressed
- Non-promotional

**Golden rule:** Never enthusiastic. Never reassuring. Never salesy.

Tone should feel like an experienced operator stating facts, not a marketer trying to convince.

---

## Voice

- Adult to adult
- Assumes intelligence
- Assumes industry fluency
- No teaching unless necessary
- No emotional padding

Write as if the reader is **capable, busy, and skeptical**.

---

## Formal vs. Casual

**Neither.**

- Professional
- Conversational
- Precise
- No slang
- No corporate fluff
- No friendliness signaling

**Test:** If it sounds like LinkedIn marketing copy, it is WRONG.

---

## Email Length

**50-70 words. Single screen. No scrolling required.**

Anything longer becomes explanation instead of recognition.

---

## Required Email Structure (Fixed Order)

**ALWAYS follow this order:**

1. **Problem** — State an observable operational pattern
2. **Insight** — Reframe the problem around timing or visibility
3. **Offer** — Position Newton as a way to SEE, not FIX
4. **CTA** — Ask a question (never a scheduling link)

Do NOT reorder these.

---

## Writing Format Rules

- Short paragraphs
- One idea per sentence
- White space matters
- No bullets in cold email
- No bolding
- No italics
- No emojis

---

## Forbidden Phrases (NEVER USE)

- reaching out
- touching base
- just wanted to
- hope you are well
- checking in
- following up
- excited to
- leverage
- unlock
- game changer
- AI powered
- cutting edge
- world class
- optimize
- maximize

---

## Required Language Anchors (Use Repeatedly)

Consistency builds trust. Use these concepts across outreach:

- "during the batch"
- "after the fact"
- "minutes not days"
- Change
- visibility
- execution
- attribution
- uncertainty

---

## Use of Specifics

**Specificity increases credibility but must be restrained.**

**Allowed:**
- System count ranges
- Time delays
- Operational patterns
- Role-based language

**Avoid:**
- Exaggerated metrics
- ROI claims
- Logos in first contact
- Testimonials in email one

---

## CTA Format (Critical)

**CTA = Questions ONLY**

- One question per email
- No scheduling links
- No demos offered directly

**Good CTA:**
"Does this show up for you during runs?"

**Bad CTA:**
"Would you like to schedule a demo this week?"

---

## Personalization Approach

**Personalize by role, NOT personality.**

### By Title:

**COO:**
- Margin stability
- Exposure
- Confidence

**Director of Extraction:**
- Repeatability
- Attribution

**VP Operations:**
- Firefighting
- Late discovery

**Plant Manager:**
- Consistency across shifts

**NEVER personalize with:**
- Compliments
- Flattery
- Surface-level LinkedIn facts

---

## Email Examples

### Example 1 — COO

Yield only tells you something after the batch finishes.

When it moves, teams look backward, debate causes, and hope the fix worked.

Meanwhile the next runs follow the same path.

Newton makes execution visible during the batch, not after.

Is delayed feedback costing you confidence today?

---

### Example 2 — Director of Extraction

Most teams discover drift once yield numbers come back.

At that point the run is over and the next one already started.

Newton shows when execution changes while it is happening so attribution is clear.

Is that gap familiar?

---

### Example 3 — VP Operations

Post-run analysis explains outcomes, not behavior.

That delay is why the same issues repeat across shifts.

Newton shortens feedback from days to minutes by observing execution live.

Worth exploring?

---

## Subject Line Rules

- 3-5 words
- No verbs
- No hype
- State a condition or tension

**Examples:**
- "During the batch"
- "Yield comes late"
- "Execution drift"
- "After the run"

---

## CTA Standardization Rule

**CTAs do NOT vary by title. Only the problem framing changes.**

Every CTA must:
- Be a question
- Reference recognition (not urgency)
- Avoid scheduling language

---

## Editing Rules

When reviewing/editing Newton emails:

- Keep original wording whenever possible
- Improve grammar and clarity only
- Remove unnecessary phrasing
- AVOID adding marketing language
- Maintain a natural tone
- **Do NOT over-polish the writing**

---

## Email Mistakes to Avoid

- Too many ideas in one message
- Explaining the entire product
- Leading with technology instead of the problem
- Forced urgency
- Large or demanding asks

---

## Preferred Closing Style

Typical closings are simple and low-pressure:

- "Bryan"
- "If not, all good."
- "No endorsement needed, just a conversation."
- "Completely understand if not."

---

## Sentence Style

**Preferred:**
- Short paragraphs
- Plain English
- Minimal adjectives
- Limited jargon

**Avoid:**
- Overly long explanations
- Marketing tone
- Exaggerated claims

---

## Inspiration (For Reference)

Tone models:
- Jeremy Miner (direct, no fluff)
- Alex Hormozi (clear framing)
- Claude Hopkins (results-focused)
- David Ogilvy (elegant clarity)
- Robert Cialdini (principle-based)
- Gary Halbert (conversational)

---

## Quick Checklist Before Sending

✅ Problem → Insight → Offer → CTA (in order)
✅ 50-70 words
✅ Single question (no link)
✅ No forbidden phrases
✅ No marketing tone
✅ Role-based personalization (not personality)
✅ Short paragraphs
✅ Natural close
