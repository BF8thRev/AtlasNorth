## d. 5 THINGS BRYAN HASN'T NOTICED

**1. The STIIIZY existing deployment is the single most valuable sales asset Bryan has — and he's not using it.**
Kennedy, Gary Vandenberghe, and Jesse Redmond are all inside an active STIIIZY deployment. STIIIZY is the most recognizable brand name in this pipeline. Every other prospect would respond immediately to "STIIIZY is already running this." But across the sales calls — Carbon Extracts, ShowMeOrganics, Curaleaf, Jared, Bud & Mary — Bryan references "California operators" generically. He never drops the STIIIZY name. That's a credibility multiplier sitting unused.

**2. The real buyer is almost never the person Bryan is talking to.**
In every serious deal: Bud & Mary (Lucas is above Zach), STIIIZY (Jesse is above Sam), iAnthus (Richie connected Kevin), Curaleaf (Deya is the actual decision layer above Mike), Ayrloom (Luke reports to someone who apparently controls $150K capital decisions). Bryan builds exceptional rapport at the operator and mid-manager level. He does not consistently map who controls the budget — and he's not asking enough "who else needs to be in this conversation" questions. The deals that stall are stalling because the actual decision maker hasn't been touched.

**3. The free 60-day pilot is being given away without commitment leverage.**
Bryan offered free 60-day trials to Carbon Extracts and ShowMeOrganics with no signed letter of intent, no post-trial pricing agreement, and no explicit conversion conversation built in. With only five systems in existence, each free pilot is a major capacity commitment. A prospect who takes a free trial without skin in the game will deprioritize it — "we'll get to it when we have bandwidth." The pilot should come with: a signed LOI, a defined evaluation criteria checklist, and a scheduled pricing conversation at Day 45, not Day 60.

**4. Prospects can't evaluate the ROI because they don't know their own waste number — and Bryan isn't helping them calculate it.**
Jared, STIIIZY/Jesse, Ayrloom's Luke, and iAnthus's Kevin all raised price as an objection. But in every case, what's actually blocking them is simpler: they don't have a real number for their yield loss or solvent inefficiency. They can't evaluate "$13K/month" against a benefit they've never measured. Bryan keeps defending the price. He should flip it: before quoting a number, ask three questions — "How many runs per day? What's your average yield %? What do you estimate you're leaving on the table?" Once the prospect calculates their own waste, they close the cost conversation themselves. Bryan is answering a question the prospect hasn't actually finished asking yet.

**5. Bryan is building a data product on top of teams that don't have data discipline — and nobody has said it out loud yet.**
Sam at STIIIZY screenshots Excel dashboards to send to executives because they won't open Excel files. Zach at Bud & Mary has no unified tracking system across four states. iAnthus uses Excel and paper logs. Jushi uses Excel. Even at Curaleaf, the question of "is this deployed in real situations" suggests they're skeptical of data quality. Newton Insights requires operators to log data consistently to generate meaningful insights. If the operators don't fill things in, the model is useless. Bryan hasn't addressed this in any of the sales calls — there's no discovery question about current data hygiene, no onboarding plan that includes data discipline, and no acknowledgment that implementation success depends on operator behavior as much as the hardware. This is the #1 reason a pilot fails silently: the product works fine, but the operator entries are sparse and inconsistent, insights are thin, and nobody can prove value. Bryan needs a data-readiness qualifier in his discovery process.
